← Radar | 📡 City Pulse — How It Works

City Pulse gives you a glanceable read on Frankfurt's real-time situation across five categories. Here's exactly how the status and trend labels are computed — and the underlying score data that proves it.

⚙️ The pipeline
Layer 1
🧮 Deterministic scoring
Active alerts are weighted by severity and service type, summed into a score per category, compared to a historical baseline, and assigned a status label. No AI involved.
No AI
→
Layer 2
🤖 AI synthesis
Gemini Flash receives the computed scores, baseline, alert bodies, and pulse history. Its only job: judge the trend per category and write the summary. It cannot assign or override status.
Gemini Flash
→
Layer 3
📤 Final output
Layer 1 status + Layer 2 trend are merged into the pulse card you see on the map. Generation frequency is adaptive — see below.
Adaptive
Status is never decided by AI. It is a score that crosses a threshold derived from historical data. The charts below show exactly where today's score sits relative to those thresholds — that is the proof.
⏱️ When is pulse generated?

The pulse runs on a schedule but skips when conditions are calm, to avoid unnecessary AI calls.

📈 How trend is judged

Trend is the only thing the AI decides. Two signals feed into it — Signal 1 is the default; Signal 2 can override it.

SignalWhat it looks atWhen it applies
Signal 1 — next-interval projection Compares the current ongoing score to the projected score at the end of the next sample interval, combined with the shape of recent history Default for all categories
Signal 2 — horizon momentum Looks at whether the horizon score series shows sharp acceleration and whether near-term activity already accounts for a large share of the total upcoming load Overrides Signal 1 only when both conditions hold; AI uses bridging language ("conditions will worsen significantly later today")

⚖️ Alert severity weights

Each active alert contributes a weight to its category score. Weights are fixed rules — not learned or adjusted by AI. RMV weights below are the base rate; they're additionally multiplied by the number of affected lines, capped at 4 — so an alert covering 4 or more lines scores the same as 4 individual alerts of the same type.
