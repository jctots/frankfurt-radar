← Radar | 📡 City Pulse — How It Works

City Pulse gives you a glanceable read on Frankfurt's real-time situation across five categories. Here's exactly how the status and trend labels are computed — and the underlying score data that proves it.

⚙️ The pipeline
Layer 1
🧮 Deterministic scoring
Active alerts are weighted by severity and service type, summed into a score per category, compared to a historical baseline, and assigned a status and trend label. Statuses de-escalate with a one-hour confirmation delay so they don't flicker. No AI involved.
No AI
→
Layer 2
🤖 AI synthesis
Gemini Flash receives the computed scores, baseline, alert bodies, and pulse history, and writes the summary and recommendation. It may correct a trend only when alert text says something the scores can't see (e.g. "service resumes at 14:30") — and must log its reason.
Gemini Flash
→
Layer 3
📤 Final output
Layer 1 status + trend (plus any logged AI content-correction) are merged into the pulse card you see on the map. Generation frequency is adaptive — see below.
Adaptive
Status and trend are never decided by AI. Status is a score crossing thresholds derived from historical data; trend compares the current score against the recent past with a noise-tolerant dead band. The charts below show exactly where today's score sits relative to those thresholds — that is the proof.
⚖️ Alert severity weights

Each active alert contributes a weight to its category score. Weights are fixed rules — not learned or adjusted by AI. RMV weights below are the base rate; they're additionally multiplied by the number of affected lines, capped at 4 — so an alert covering 4 or more lines scores the same as 4 individual alerts of the same type.

🎯 How status labels are assigned

Computed from the score vs. a historical baseline. The baseline deliberately excludes the most recent hours, so a disruption that is still happening can't redefine "normal" and hide itself. 100% deterministic.

StatusConditionWhat it means
clearScore = 0No active alerts
minorScore at or below the typical levelActivity at or below the baseline for this category
moderateBetween the typical level and the 75th percentileElevated above normal — worth being aware of
severeAbove the 75th percentileWell above what this category normally looks like
Two safeguards on top of the thresholds. Weather floor: official DWD warning levels override the baseline — a severe (level 3) warning always shows at least moderate, an extreme (level 4) warning always shows severe, even when recent history is calm. De-escalation delay: a status only steps down after staying lower for a full extra hour, so it can't flicker between labels at a threshold boundary. Escalations always apply immediately.
📈 How trend is judged

Trend is computed deterministically, just like status. Three rules feed into it, in order:

RuleWhat it looks atWhen it applies
Score vs. recent past Compares the current score to the average of the 3 preceding sample intervals, with a ±15% dead band so small fluctuations read as stable Default for all categories
Scheduled surge Alerts with known future start times (storm warnings, planned closures, events): when significant scheduled activity is both large relative to the typical level and mostly imminent, the trend escalates one step Escalation only — the AI uses bridging language ("calm now, but a storm system arrives tonight")
AI content correction Alert text occasionally states a direction the scores can't see — "service resumes at 14:30", "conditions will intensify overnight". The AI may correct the trend for that category, quoting the text as its logged reason Rare; only when alert content explicitly contradicts the computed trend
⏱️ When is pulse generated?

The pulse runs on a schedule but skips when conditions are calm, to avoid unnecessary AI calls.
